# Campaign Salvage Report

Analysis of all 10 inherited campaigns — what happened, what to keep, what to ditch, and what the pattern is.

## The Headline Pattern

**10 out of 10 campaigns are transactional.** Every single one tried to change behavior with points, discounts, or bonuses. Not one of them tried to change how customers *felt* about the brand or themselves. That's the whole story in one line — and it's why the program is "fine but forgettable."

Several campaigns' OWN post-mortems gesture toward identity-driven fixes ("coffee personality," "feels like Basecamp"), but nobody ever built one.

## Campaign-by-Campaign Breakdown

### 01 — Double Points Weekend ❌
- 23% lift during promo, gone within 3 days. ~$2,400 in rewards liability. No retention lift.
- Classic "discount treadmill" — paid more for behavior that would have happened anyway.
- **Salvage:** Nothing. Money pit.

### 02 — Happy Hour Points Boost ❌
- 8% afternoon lift; 34% of push notifications dismissed; customer complaints about spam timing.
- Discount can't fix mindshare — customers aren't skipping afternoons over price, they're not thinking about the brand at all.
- **Salvage:** Nothing.

### 03 — Birthday Bonus ⚠️
- 62% redemption, 31% email open rate. BUT 73% of redeemers were already regulars; zero lapsed-member reactivation.
- Preached to the converted. Industry-standard, non-differentiating.
- **Salvage:** Keep as baseline hygiene. Reimagine around identity — a personal barista note, a co-created drink — not bonus points.

### 04 — Refer-a-Friend ⚠️
- Never launched. Died in legal review + dev deprioritization.
- Doc itself notes the conceptual gap: rewards to refer, no *reason* to refer.
- **Salvage:** Keep the referral infrastructure for later. Pair it with a shareable identity hook (the doc even names "coffee personality quiz" as a stronger alternative — that's twice now).

### 05 — Early Bird Bonus ❌
- Killed at draft stage. Team's own note: "backwards thinking" — subsidizing behavior already happening.
- Key insight (worth keeping): 83% of members don't engage at all — that's where the work is.
- **Salvage:** The insight, not the campaign.

### 06 — Seasonal Drinks 2x ⚠️
- Trial rose to 31% (from 22% baseline), but only 12% repeat.
- The NEW DRINK drove trial. The points were redundant spend. No matching drinks to taste.
- **Salvage:** Ditch the points layer. Pivot toward preference-based recommendations — "If you like X, you'll love Y."

### 07 — App Download Bonus ❌ (mechanic) / ⚠️ (app)
- ~400 downloads/month, 69% never make a second transaction. High cost-per-engaged-member.
- Paying for downloads, not engagement. App has no reason to reopen.
- **Salvage:** Ditch the bonus. Fix the app — internal notes hint at personalization / "coffee personality" as the real fix.

### 08 — Survey Bonus ⚠️
- 23% completion. Minimal cost. Useful qualitative data.
- Surveys collect data, they don't create engagement. And the data kept surfacing the same diagnosis nobody acted on.
- **Salvage:** The *feedback*, not the engagement mechanic. Feed the strategy, not a rewards line item.

### 09 — Social Share ❌
- 12 redemptions total. Zero organic engagement. Customers felt embarrassed, staff found verification annoying.
- Can't buy authentic advocacy. Nothing about "I earned 20 points" is shareable.
- Telling detail from the notes: identity-driven moments ("I'm an Espresso Explorer!") are what people actually share.
- **Salvage:** Nothing from the mechanic. Everything from that observation.

### 10 — Visit Streak Bonus ❌
- Killed in draft. Gaming risk, complex, unclear audience, "hollow Duolingo imitation."
- Streaks work when the underlying behavior has intrinsic value (learning). Daily coffee doesn't.
- The author's "Parting Thought" is the gold: *make people feel like this is THEIR place.*
- **Salvage:** The parting thought. A thematic turning point in the campaign set.

## What Actually Survives

**Keep as infrastructure:**
- Birthday trigger (but reimagine the content)
- Referral infrastructure (but needs a real hook)
- Survey cadence (for data, not engagement)
- App itself (if we give it a reason to exist)

**Ditch entirely:**
- Every pure points-multiplier campaign
- Push-notification spam mechanics
- Pay-for-download bribes
- Pay-for-sharing tactics
- Streaks on a behavior with no intrinsic value

---

## The Meta-Discovery

Read those campaigns back to back and one thing becomes unmistakable: the previous manager's team kept trying engagement tactics, but every single campaign was about getting MORE TRANSACTIONS — not about making people FEEL something.

Even more striking: several of the campaign docs — written by the same team that built the transactional campaigns — gesture at the real fix. "Coffee personality." "Feels like Basecamp, not corporate rewards." "Make people feel like this is THEIR place." The answer has been hiding in their own notes the entire time.

That's consistent with the customer feedback ("no personality," "transactional") and the flyer (generic, no brand). **The program doesn't need better points. It needs identity.**
