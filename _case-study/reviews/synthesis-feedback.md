# Synthesis Feedback — Three Perspectives

Three advisors reviewed `analysis/comprehensive-research-synthesis.md` from their own lenses. Here's what each one sees.

---

## (ಠ_ಠ) Exec — Leadership / ROI Perspective

### The core problem

Forget "personality." The core problem is we're burning **$14.50 to acquire a member worth $29** — and falling. That's a 2x payback collapsing toward 1x. ROI went from 1.8 to 0.5 in six months. MAU down 60% while signups are up 123% means we're paying to fill a leaky bucket — the worst unit economics in the deck. In 1–2 months the program goes net-negative. Nobody else in this synthesis is saying that out loud.

What I care about that others don't: **the $40K is gone.** Sunk. The question isn't "was it wasted" — it's *"does dollar 40,001 earn its keep in 90 days."* Everything downstream of that is a feelings exercise.

### What we do about it

Bias for action, tight leash. I'm not funding a "coffee identity" platform rebuild on a quiz and a vibe.

- **Fund Play #2 — barista memory/training — this week.** Near-zero capex, tests the relationship hypothesis cheap.
- **Pilot Play #1 (personality quiz + matched recs) in two stores.** 60-day window, capped at $15K.

**Success in 90 days:** MAU recovers to 800+ (from 480); 30-day retention back above 38%; cost-per-member under $11; program NPS above 25. Miss two of four and we sunset the program and redirect spend to core brand (NPS 67 is the only asset we still own).

No more "points can't carry an empty identity" poetry. **Numbers or it dies.**

## (◠‿◠) Product Designer — UX / Experience Perspective

### The core problem

Basecamp doesn't have a loyalty problem. It has an **identity vacuum.**

Picture a member opening the app. What do they see? A number. What does that number say about them? Nothing. Who are they inside this program? A balance. That's the whole emotional arc — and it's why people drift.

*"Indifference is the product"* is the truest line in the synthesis, because indifference isn't something members *feel about* the program — it's what the program *gives them.* The program hands members a blank mirror and asks them to get excited about their own reflection.

Contrast that with the quote that should haunt leadership: *"Dutch Bros remembers my name. Starbucks at least has games."* Members aren't describing features. They're describing **being seen** and **being played with.** The two deepest loyalty emotions there are. Basecamp offers neither.

### What we do about it

Give every member a **self** inside Basecamp.

The coffee-personality quiz isn't a gimmick — it's the first moment the program looks back at them. "You're a Midnight Wanderer. Here are three drinks built for you. Here's a pin." Suddenly points have narrative, tiers become chapters, and the barista has something warm to say.

**The moments that matter:**
- **The naming moment** — "You're an Espresso Explorer" lands like a tiny horoscope. People screenshot those.
- **The recognition moment** — barista glances at a note, says "the usual, Midnight Wanderer?" That's a story they'll tell.
- **The artifact moment** — a pin, a sticker, a patch on their laptop. Identity you can wear.
- **The discovery moment** — "you've never tried this, and we think it's you." Being known beats being rewarded.

Stop selling points. Start selling **a version of themselves they like.**

## (•‿•) Barista Lead — Ground-Level / Operations Perspective

### The core problem

Your spreadsheet says "indifference is the product" and honestly? That tracks. Because here's what customers actually say to me, every single shift:

- *"What should I try today?"*
- *"What's good?"*
- *"I always get the same thing, surprise me."*
- *"What do YOU drink?"*

You know what nobody asks me? "How many points do I have?" "What tier am I?" "When's my next reward?" In four years, I can count those conversations on one hand. The app? Customers hand me their phone and ask ME to figure it out. That's not a loyalty program — that's homework we assigned them.

Marcus stays because I know he wants oat milk, no foam, extra hot. Sarah stays because we talked about her trying the Ethiopian last week. **That's the real program, and we've been ignoring it while building an app nobody opens.**

### What we do about it

The coffee personality idea? That's the first thing in this whole document that sounds like a real conversation. Because it IS the conversation we're already having. "Bold espresso person or smooth latte person?" I have that exact exchange ten times a day.

- **The quiz / taste profile.** Give me something to hand customers when they ask "what should I try?" A card, a QR code, whatever. Turn the question I already answer into their entry point.
- **The regulars notebook.** A thousand times yes. Let me write "Marcus — oat, no foam, trying new single origins" somewhere the next shift can see. That's not tech, that's a notebook. Could start Monday.
- **Archetype pins / stickers.** Dutch Bros stickers are on every water bottle in town. Customers WANT to flex their coffee identity. "Espresso Explorer" on a pin? Regulars would actually wear that.

**What I'd skip:** anything that adds app friction, anything that makes me explain "tiers" to a confused customer at 7:45am with a line out the door.

Does this feel like Basecamp? The personality play does. Points never did.

---

## Where They Converge

All three land on the **Coffee Personality** idea — but from wildly different angles:

- **Exec:** fund it as a tight, measurable pilot. Two stores, 90 days, numbers or it dies.
- **Product Designer:** it's the first moment the program sees the member back. Identity is the product.
- **Barista Lead:** it's a real conversation we're already having — give us the tools to capture it.

The synthesis said "the fix is personality." All three advisors agree — but each one tells you something different about **why**, and **how to prove it**.
